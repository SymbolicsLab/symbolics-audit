## Source
Direct conversation — not a paper or submission. Theory session exploring the entanglement thesis from the Nature of Distinction chapter and its connection to the two-body condition.

## Theory decisions made

### 1. Entanglement is constitutive
All cuts are symmetric. Both poles are co-constituted by the same act. The standalone appearance (one pole self-sufficient, the other residual) is produced by fold/naturalization, not by the cut. Entanglement is the default condition; standalone is the achievement.

### 2. Entanglement grounds the two-body condition
The two-body condition is derivable from entanglement. Distinction is constitutively relational (a boundary requiring two sides). At emergence, the two sides are two bodies. The two-body condition is not just an empirical claim about development — it's a structural consequence of what distinction IS.

### 3. Bodies and poles are mutually constitutive
At the moment of distinction-emergence, the field-cut (poles) and the agent-cut (roles) are one event. The poles can't exist without the bodies (a boundary for no one isn't a boundary). The bodies can't become a communicative pair without the poles (no distinction, no roles). One act, one structure, two levels.

### 4. Distinction is constitutively binary
No three-way distinctions exist. A boundary has exactly two sides. Multi-way partitions are always compositions of binary cuts. The binariness, dyadicity, and entanglement are three descriptions of one fact.

### 5. The field is always continuous
No pre-existing joints. What look like sharp joints are high-resilience index fits, not domain properties. Resilience is the measure, not topology. This was established earlier in the session during the Synthese Social Science integration, and entanglement provides the deeper grounding: if all joints are index-relative, then all complements are produced, and entanglement is universal.
